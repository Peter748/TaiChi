Yang Family Lineage
杨氏太极拳传承 · The Yang Family Tree · Chen Village → Newcastle

Chen Changxing (陳長興, 1771–1853)
14th generation master of Chen-style Tai Chi in Chen Village, Henan Province. Broke with tradition by accepting Yang Luchan — an outsider — as a full indoor student, a decision that would change the course of Tai Chi history.

1st Generation — Yang Luchan (楊露禪, 1799–1872)
Founder of Yang-style Tai Chi. A peasant from Hebei Province who mastered Chen-style after years of dedicated study in Chen Village. Became so formidable he earned the name Yang the Invincible (楊無敵). In 1850 he was appointed by the Qing Imperial Court to teach the elite Manchu Palace Battalion in Beijing — the first time Tai Chi was taught outside the Chen family. His decision to teach non-family members began the worldwide spread of the art.

2nd Generation — Yang Banhou (楊班候, 1837–1892)
Second son of Yang Luchan and Imperial martial arts instructor. Known for fierce, demanding teaching and explosive internal power (fa jin). Practised a compact small-frame style. His most famous student, Wu Quanyou, went on to co-found Wu-style Tai Chi. Yang Banhou is regarded as one of the most formidable martial artists of his era.

2nd Generation — Yang Jianhou (楊健候, 1839–1917)
Third son of Yang Luchan. Where his brother Banhou was fierce, Jianhou was known for a gentle, soft approach and a middle-frame style now recognised as the Secret Transmission of Yang Jianhou. He refined the art significantly and passed it to both his sons — including Yang Chengfu, who would bring it to the world.

3rd Generation — Yang Chengfu (楊澄甫, 1883–1936)
Son of Yang Jianhou and the defining figure of modern Yang-style Tai Chi. Developed the large-frame (大架) style practised worldwide today — removing vigorous fa jin, jumps and stamping from the solo form to make it accessible for health, while retaining them in weapons forms. His nationwide teaching tours across China and landmark 1931 book The Essence and Applications of Taijiquan established Yang-style as the world's most popular Tai Chi form. The Yang 28 forms practised at Golden Lotus Tai Chi trace directly to his teaching.

4th Generation — Fu Zhongwen (傅鍾文, 1903–1994)
Began training under Yang Chengfu at the age of 9, accompanying him on all teaching tours across China — Yang Chengfu would teach, Fu Zhongwen would demonstrate. He became family by marrying Zou Kuei Cheng, great-granddaughter of Yang Jianhou. Renowned as an undefeatable Push Hands competitor, he was voted one of the One Hundred Living Treasures of China. In 1944 he founded the Yongnian Tai Chi Association in Shanghai — still active worldwide. In 1963 the PRC published his definitive book Yang Family Tai Chi Chuan. He dedicated his life to teaching Tai Chi freely to all who wished to learn.

5th Generation — Fu Sheng Yuan (傅聲遠, 1931–2017)
Only son of Fu Zhongwen. Born in Guangfu — the birthplace of Yang Family Tai Chi. Began training at age 7, tutored for nearly half a century by his father and the last surviving disciples of Yang Chengfu and Yang Jianhou — often training from 11pm for four or more hours each night. As the first male of his generation he became the 5th Generation patriarch of the Yang family lineage. In 1989 Fu Sheng Yuan emigrated to Perth, Australia, welcomed by Prime Minister Bob Hawke, and established the Fu Sheng Yuan Tai Chi Academy. He spent the rest of his life spreading authentic Yang-style Tai Chi to over 25 countries. Awarded the Chinese Wushu Association's highest honour — 8th Dan (Silver Dragon Badge) — the only person outside China to receive this distinction. Passed away 27 March 2017.

6th Generation — Damon Bramich (Fu Wu Xai, b.1969)
Master Teacher and principal of Newcastle Tai Chi. Began Tai Chi at age 12 and started his formal apprenticeship under Fu Sheng Yuan in 1991 — at just 21, already holding black belts in three styles of karate and a Black Sash in Choy Lee Fut. Over 26 years of direct training he became Fu Sheng Yuan's leading BaShi (close-door) disciple, travelling on over 50 tours across China, Australia and Asia alongside his teacher. In his teacher's final years Damon provided personal care and received Fu Sheng Yuan's final teachings over two weeks before his passing in March 2017 — making him the first non-family lineage successor. Awarded 6th Dan by the Chinese Wushu Association (2008) and World Champion in Tai Chi and Wushu across six countries. Also created the Yang 28 Competition Form practised at Golden Lotus Tai Chi.

Peter — Golden Lotus Tai Chi, Newcastle
Trained under Damon Bramich, continuing this direct lineage from Yang Luchan through Fu Sheng Yuan to Newcastle, Australia. Peter teaches Yang-style Tai Chi and Yang Push Hands (推手, Tuī Shǒu) at Golden Lotus Tai Chi. Push Hands is the partner practice of Tai Chi — two practitioners maintain contact and apply the principles of yielding, listening (听劲 tīng jìn), neutralising and redirecting force. It bridges the solo form and martial application, developing sensitivity, timing and the ability to remain rooted while responding fluidly to an incoming force.

Yang 28 — Competition Form
杨氏太极拳 (Yángshì Tàijíquán) · Australian Competition Form

This Competition Form was created by Damon Bramich specifically for Australian Tai Chi competition, designed to fit within the standard 5-minute solo time limit. It distils the essence of the Yang style long form into 38 flowing postures that showcase the characteristic large, open movements and even tempo of the Yang tradition.

Yang-style Tai Chi traces its origins to Yang Luchan (1799–1872), a peasant farmer from Yongnian, Hebei Province. After spending 18 years studying at the Chen family village under master Chen Changxing, Yang Luchan moved to Beijing where he became famous for never losing a challenge match, earning the title "Yang the Invincible" (杨无敌). In 1850 he was invited to teach the Imperial Guard, beginning Tai Chi's spread from a village family art to a worldwide practice.

His grandson Yang Chengfu (1883–1936) standardised the style into the large, slow, graceful form practised today, removing explosive fa jin movements in favour of continuous, meditative flow. This made the art accessible to millions and remains the world's most widely practised martial art style.

Yang 28 — Standard Form
杨氏太极拳二十八式 · Standard Training Form · 5 min 15 sec

The Standard Form shares the same Yang Chengfu lineage as the Competition Form but follows a fuller sequence at a more relaxed pace of 5 minutes 15 seconds. It is used as the primary teaching and practice form at Newcastle Tai Chi, providing students with additional time to settle into each posture and develop internal awareness.

The Standard Form includes the full Golden Rooster Stands on One Leg sequence on both sides, giving students longer exposure to balance training and weight shifting — key hallmarks of Yang-style practice.

Taiji Qigong ShiBaShi — Set 1
太极气功十八式 · Tàijí Qìgōng Shí Bā Shì · Created 1979 · Professor Lin Housheng

ShiBaShi was created in 1979 by Professor Lin Housheng at the Shanghai Institute of Traditional Chinese Medicine. It synthesises Yang-style Tai Chi with traditional Qigong breathing, making it accessible to all ages and fitness levels. It is one of the most widely practised Qigong routines in the world.

The routine begins in Wuji Stance and closes in Holding an Energy Ball Stance (palms facing the lower Dan Tian). Breathing throughout is abdominal — inhale through the nose expanding the lower belly, exhale contracting it.

Movements 11–13 (Scooping from the Sea, Playing with Waves, Spreading Your Wings) are performed first on the left side then repeated on the right side before continuing to Movement 14.

Taiji Qigong ShiBaShi — Set 2
太极气功十八式第二套 · Created 1988 · Lin Housheng & He Wei-Qi

ShiBaShi Set 2 was created in 1988 by Professor Lin Housheng in collaboration with Master He Wei-Qi. Where Set 1 focuses on opening the meridians of the arms — which is why many practitioners feel Qi in their palms after practising — Set 2 focuses on opening the meridians of the legs, connecting with earth energy and directing it upward through the body.

The movements are slightly more physically demanding than Set 1, with more pronounced leg flexion and weight transfer. This earth energy is pumped by the legs, directed through the waist, and expressed through the arms — circulating effectively through every joint and meridian. Practitioners often notice improved balance and a deeper sense of grounding after regular practice of Set 2.

The 18 movements in order: Peacock unfolds its tail · Heavenly roll · Breeze blowing at the willow tree · Needle at sea bottom · Fisherman casting net · Dragon circles the mountain · Heel kick to the front · Warrior in meditation · Yellow dragon clawing · Shooting arrow · Two dragons flying out to sea · The waves lapping or rolling · The lion playing the ball · Carrying the moon towards your body · Phoenix spreading its wings · Hit tiger's ears · Farmer grinds the corn · Press the palms in calmness.

Qigong Lotus Form
莲花气功 · Liánhuā Qìgōng · Jade Lady Tai Chi · Suzette Hosken 2021

The Lotus Qigong was created by Suzette Hosken of Jade Lady Tai Chi (jadeladytaichi.com.au). The form draws its imagery from the lotus flower — one of the most profound symbols in Chinese and Buddhist culture, representing purity, awakening and resilience: the lotus rises from muddy water to bloom in perfect beauty, undisturbed by the conditions around it.

The form begins and ends in Wuji stance — the state of open emptiness before movement — bookending the sequence with stillness. Each of the 14 movements flows into the next without repetition, giving the form a meditative, continuous quality distinct from the repetitive structure of Shibashi.

The 14 movements in order: Wuji opening · Waving hands in the air · Turtle treads water · Rabbit digs the earth · Fair maiden sprinkles water on the young lotus · Young lotus blossoms · Turn to press the wind left then right · Lift to the sky · Press to the earth · White crane spreads wings left then right · Rabbit digs the earth · Waving hands in the air · Wuji closing.

The animal imagery — turtle, rabbit, white crane — reflects the ancient Chinese tradition of observing nature's creatures to understand health and graceful movement. The white crane is a symbol of longevity and spiritual elevation appearing across Tai Chi and Qigong traditions for over a thousand years.

Yang Sabre — Dao (刀)
杨氏太极刀 · Yángshì Tàijí Dāo · Single-edged curved blade

The Dao (sabre or broadsword) is a single-edged curved blade and one of the four major traditional Chinese weapons alongside the spear, staff and straight sword. In Yang-style Tai Chi, the Dao form is traditionally taught before the Jian (straight sword), as it develops the basic blocking angles, power training and stepping patterns that form the prerequisite for the more refined Jian techniques.

The Yang family Dao form was documented by Chen Yen-lin in 1932 based on writings compiled by Wu Ho-ching, a student of Yang Luchan. The form features dramatic sweeping cuts, thrusts and deflections that express a more martial character than the bare-hand forms, while still embodying the Tai Chi principles of yielding, redirecting and circular motion.

At Newcastle Tai Chi the Dao is practised over 2 minutes — 23 postures that take the practitioner through the full range of sabre techniques while maintaining the flowing, connected quality of Yang-style movement.

Yang Sword — Jian (剑)
杨氏太极剑 · Yángshì Tàijí Jiàn · The Gentleman of Weapons

The Jian (straight double-edged sword) is known in Chinese martial lore as "The Gentleman of Weapons" and "The King of Short Weapons". Unlike the powerful chopping action of the Dao, the Jian relies on subtle deflections, precise thrusting and the ability to stick to and redirect an opponent's blade — a perfect physical expression of Tai Chi's yielding philosophy.

The Yang and Wu families taught Jian technique to Qing dynasty military officers, and the traditional Yang Jian form has been passed down largely intact through the generations. The straight sword was historically associated with scholarly refinement and Daoist spiritual practice — Daoist priests used the Jian in ceremonies to cut through veils of illusion and banish negative influences.

Mastering the Jian is considered the highest expression of Yang-style weapons training. Its 29 postures — practised over 2 minutes 5 seconds at Newcastle Tai Chi — demand precise coordination, sensitivity and an understanding of the sword as an extension of the body's Qi rather than merely a physical implement.

The 13 fundamental Jian techniques (striking, thrusting, deflecting, slicing, pressing, lifting, carrying, intercepting, cutting, pointing, sweeping, blocking and circling) underpin all movements of the form and connect it directly to the 13 original principles of Tai Chi itself.

Ba Duan Jin — Eight Section Brocade
八段锦 · Bāduànjǐn · Eight Pieces of Silk Brocade · Song Dynasty c.960–1279 CE

Ba Duan Jin is one of the oldest and most widely practised Qigong forms in the world, with a history of over 1,000 years. Ba (八) means eight, Duan (段) means sections, and Jin (锦) means silk brocade — the name reflects both the eight movements and the silken, flowing quality they should embody. After practising the sequence, one should feel as though wearing the finest silk.

References appear in Song Dynasty medical manuals (960–1279 CE), and the form is traditionally attributed to the military general Yue Fei, who is said to have taught these exercises to his soldiers to build physical and mental resilience. The form was further developed through Daoist and Shaolin monastic lineages and has been practised continuously for centuries across China and East Asia.

In 2003 the Chinese Health Qigong Association standardised the modern standing version, which is now the most widely taught. Each of the eight movements targets a specific organ system and meridian network according to Traditional Chinese Medicine.

Health benefits by movement:

1. Two hands hold up the heavens — Regulates the Triple Burner (Sanjiao), activates overall Qi circulation, balances energy throughout the body.

2. Draw the bow to shoot the eagle — Strengthens the Kidneys and Spleen, opens the chest, strengthens the lower body through the horse stance.

3. Separate heaven and earth — Regulates the Spleen and Stomach, benefits the Liver and Gallbladder, aids digestion.

4. Wise owl gazes backwards — Eliminates the five strains and seven impairments, relieves neck and shoulder tension, strengthens the optic nerve, calms the mind.

5. Sway the head and shake the tail — Removes Heart fire (excess energy/stress), cools and calms the entire system, loosens the spine and hips.

6. Two hands hold the feet — Strengthens the Kidneys and lower back, stretches the entire posterior chain, stimulates the bladder meridian.

7. Clench the fists and glare fiercely — Increases overall Qi and physical strength, stimulates the Liver meridian, builds vitality and inner power.

8. Bouncing on the toes — Stimulates all meridians and the Du Mai (Governing Vessel), massages the Kidney 1 (Bubbling Spring) point on the soles, said to eliminate one hundred diseases.

Five Element Qigong
五行气功 · Wǔ Xíng Qìgōng · Wood · Fire · Earth · Metal · Water

Five Element Qigong is rooted in one of the oldest frameworks of Chinese philosophy and medicine — the Five Phases (五行, Wǔ Xíng). Unlike Ba Duan Jin or Shibashi, which are standardised forms, Five Element Qigong exists in many versions across different schools, all sharing the same underlying theory: five movements, one for each element, performed in sequence to harmonise the internal organs and balance the body's energetic system.

The five elements — Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal and Water — are not static substances but dynamic phases of energy in constant transformation. Each element corresponds to a season, a direction, an organ pair, an emotion, and a movement quality. The Taoist philosophers observed these patterns in nature and mapped them onto the human body to understand health, illness, and healing.

Each element and its practice:

Wood (木 Mù) — Spring · East · Liver & Gallbladder
Rising, expansive movement like a tree growing upward. Cultivates patience, flexibility and creativity. Addresses anger, frustration and stagnation. The movement quality is upward.

Fire (火 Huǒ) — Summer · South · Heart & Small Intestine
Opening, outward movement that ignites inner warmth. Cultivates joy, clarity and enthusiasm. Addresses anxiety, excessive excitement and confusion. The movement quality is outward and expansive.

Earth (土 Tǔ) — Late Summer · Centre · Spleen & Stomach
Grounded, centred lifting and lowering. Cultivates stability, nourishment and groundedness. Addresses worry, overthinking and doubt. The movement quality is centred and stable.

Metal (金 Jīn) — Autumn · West · Lungs & Large Intestine
Gathering, contracting movement from wide to concentrated. Cultivates clarity, precision and letting go. Addresses grief, sadness and holding on. The movement quality is inward and condensing.

Water (水 Shuǐ) — Winter · North · Kidneys & Bladder
Sinking and flowing movement, like water finding its level. Cultivates wisdom, willpower and fearlessness. Addresses fear, exhaustion and lack of will. The movement quality is downward and flowing.

Each exercise is typically repeated 5 or 6 times, and the whole cycle can be repeated 3–5 times. It is recommended to practice in alignment with the seasons — emphasising the Wood exercise in spring, Fire in summer, and so on — though the complete cycle is beneficial year-round.

Yang Style 36 Fan
杨式太极扇三十六式 · Yángshì Tàijí Shàn · 4 Sections · 2 Minutes

The Yang Style 36 Fan form is a weapons form that combines the flowing elegance of Yang-style Tai Chi with the expressive beauty of the traditional Chinese folding fan. The fan (扇, shàn) has been used in Chinese martial arts for centuries — simultaneously a performance implement, a defensive tool and a symbol of refinement and cultural sophistication.

The 36 movements are organised into four sections (段, duàn), each building in complexity and expression. The poetic movement names draw on rich imagery from Chinese nature, mythology and history — the phoenix, the black dragon, Lady Zhaojun, the golden oriole, wild geese flying south — giving the practitioner a vivid internal image to animate each posture.

Section 1 (第一段, 1–9): Opening posture through Turn body and strike the tiger — establishes the fundamental fan techniques and footwork.

Section 2 (第二段, 10–18): Divine dragon turns its head through Cross step and upward strike — introduces turning and flowing transitions.

Section 3 (第三段, 19–27): Raise arms and admire flowers through Turn body and admire flowers — sweeping, expressive arm movements with the open fan.

Section 4 (第四段, 28–36): Overlord lifts the cauldron through Closing posture — powerful concluding movements ending in stillness.

The form is practised over 2 minutes — 36 postures flowing continuously over 3 minutes 33 seconds — approximately 6 seconds per posture — with the characteristic Yang-style smoothness, each named movement a small poem in motion.

Yang Jian — Long Form
杨氏太极剑长套 · 67 Postures · 3 Minutes 16 Seconds

The Yang Jian Long Form is the extended expression of Yang-style straight sword practice — 67 postures flowing over 3 minutes 16 seconds, approximately 3 seconds per movement. Where the Short Form provides an accessible introduction to the Jian, the Long Form demands a deeper mastery of the sword's 13 fundamental techniques across a far wider range of scenarios and transitions.

The poetic movement names draw richly on Chinese mythology and nature — the swallow, phoenix, green dragon, black dragon, white tiger, rhinoceros, roc, mustang, white ape — each name a vivid image to animate the technique. Recurring movements like Little Dipper, Tiger Covers its Head, Shooting the Wild Goose and Falling Flowers appear multiple times in alternating directions, developing bilateral coordination and rhythmic flow.

Highlights of the form include Fishing for the Moon at the Sea Bottom (42), Fish Jumps Over the Dragon Gate (60), Black Dragon Twists Around the Pole (61–62), and the closing sequence culminating in Return the Sword to its Original Position (67) — a return to stillness that mirrors the opening Prepare posture.
